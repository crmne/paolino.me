Everyone in tech is talking about taste. "Taste is the new skill." "In the age of AI, taste is all that matters." I agree with half of it. But most people get the mechanism wrong.

They treat taste like it's an innate gift. Some people just *have it*. A mystical eye for quality.

That's not what taste is. Taste is the residue of experience. You develop it by doing things, making things, struggling with things, and understanding things. Nobody has taste in music they've never listened to. Nobody has taste in code they've never written.

An ex of mine used to say she hated jazz. She'd never listened to jazz. She didn't hate it — she had zero exposure to it, and without exposure, everything sounded the same. Noise without meaning. That's what no taste looks like. Not bad judgment. *No* judgment. You can't evaluate what you've never experienced.

I played jazz guitar for years. I can hear when a note is *wrong* but played on purpose — naughty, playful, alive. That recognition is instant. It's felt, not reasoned. It comes from thousands of hours of doing. There's no shortcut to it.

---

I built an open source library that people love. Appreciation posts on Reddit. People saying it *feels right* — the API, the abstractions, the developer experience. I put over a thousand hours into it. Not because I'm slow. Because taste takes time. You try something, it doesn't feel right, you try something else. You read other people's code and think *yes, this is how it should work* or *no, this is fighting the grain*. You accumulate a sense for what good looks like, and you apply it relentlessly.

Here's what's happening when people say they love it: they feel *seen*. Someone made choices that reflect something they believe but never articulated. Taste takes a lifetime of internalized, unspoken experience and compresses it into a product. And when someone with a similar enough life encounters it, they recognize it instantly. Not intellectually. In their gut.

That's not craftsmanship. That's love.

---

Now here's where LLMs get interesting. People say AI has no taste. That's not quite right. An LLM has a *million* tastes. It's seen every style, every pattern, every aesthetic ever committed to text or code. The problem isn't that it can't taste. It's that it can't *pick*.

I've been trying to get an LLM to make a beautiful website. It generates options. It can even tell me "this is much better!" But I look at the output and think: this is ugly. I point it to other sites. *Look at why these work.* It can't see it. A million references and no conviction.

Picking isn't cognitive. It's felt. You don't *decide* what's good. You *recognize* it — with your body, not your brain. That recognition is the compressed output of every website you've ever used, every book you've held, every room you've walked into that felt right or wrong. Your entire life, in a millisecond of judgment.

---

So here's the trap. Production is becoming free. You can generate a codebase, a website, a business plan — in minutes. The natural instinct: great, I can stop doing things myself.

If taste comes from doing, and you stop doing, you lose your taste. And without taste, you can't pick. You accept the first thing that doesn't obviously suck. You ask for a faster horse, every time.

Worse: when everyone without experience prompts the same machine, they converge on the same output. Everything starts looking the same, reading the same, feeling the same. Not bad. Just boring. Indistinguishable. Because without lived experience shaping the ask, every prompt is basically the same prompt.

The people who stand out will be the ones with something specific to say. Something shaped by a weird, particular, unrepeatable life. That's always been true. It's about to matter a lot more.

---

Here's the counterintuitive part: this is the best moment in history to develop taste. Learning has never been cheaper. You can try ten approaches to a problem before lunch. You can build a working prototype in an afternoon. The tools for accumulating experience are better than they've ever been.

Use AI to accelerate the *exposure*, not to replace it. Learn faster, not less. Build more, understand more — at a pace no previous generation could match.

We're heading toward a split. Not between people who use AI and people who don't. Between people who use AI to *learn* and people who use AI to *avoid learning*. Taste is what separates them.

You will still have to know things. What's changed is you've never had better tools to learn.

Use them.
