I started founding a company in Germany in late January. It is now late June.

In that time, the state, two courts, a notary, a law firm, a tax firm, and a string of software vendors have all found a way to bill me. Every single one of them, on time.

I have spent more than 9,600 euros to start a company: a little over 7,600 in fees and bills, plus 2,000 in share capital frozen in an account I am not allowed to touch. And after almost five months, here is what I have to show for it:

**I have not been able to send a single invoice of my own.**

Not one.

Everyone in this story could invoice me. I am the only one who can't invoice anyone.

## "Just send your customers an invoice"

This is the first thing people say. You have two companies in the register. Just bill someone.

I can't. Most of my clients are outside Germany. Cross-border B2B in Europe runs on reverse charge, and reverse charge needs a VAT ID. No VAT ID, no clean invoice. I asked for it on 29 May. It still has not come.

"But you also coach people here in Germany," people say. "Invoice them." I could. A domestic invoice does not strictly need the VAT ID today. But the moment it arrives, I would have to reissue every one of those invoices with the VAT ID on them. Bill once now, bill again later, for a couple hundred euros of coaching income. So I wait. The correct way to invoice a client in Germany, it turns out, is to not invoice them yet.

## I cannot even accept a thank-you

This is the part that finally broke my patience.

I maintain open-source software under my own name. [RubyLLM](https://rubyllm.com) and others. People and companies sponsor that work to say thanks. Separately, a company offered to support RubyLLM directly and asked, reasonably, for an invoice.

I have a company now. I just paid thousands of euros to create it. So I proposed the obvious thing to my tax advisors: I keep owning my projects personally, transfer no copyright, and simply grant the company the right to handle the commercial side, the sponsorships and related work. One clean structure, through the company I already built.

No.

Because my GitHub account is in my name as a person, the income has to be taxed personally, through "a separate commercial structure." There is, I was told, "no alternative under current law." If I want the money to flow through my company, my company needs its own separate GitHub account. Until then, to accept gratitude for free software, I would have to register yet another business.

I built a company specifically so I could operate as a company. The advice, in good faith and probably correct, is that I should bolt a second, personal registration onto the side just to receive a few hundred dollars a month in thank-you payments for open source.

I have two companies. Accepting a thank-you, it turns out, needs a third.

The system did not say no to the money. It said: to take it cleanly, build more bureaucracy.

## My company name was "too generic"

I wanted to call the company Plenty. It says what I want my software to be: simple, immediate, beautiful, never more complicated than it needs to be. One word, the whole idea.

The register would not take it. German company names have to be "distinctive" under the commercial code, and a plain descriptive word does not qualify. So began a hunt for a word the state would allow:

- **Plenty.** A simple English term, they said. It translates to *reichlich*. Rejected.
- **Plenty Group.** Two simple English terms. Rejected.
- **Plenty Labs.** "Labs" is *also* a simple English term. Rejected.
- **Plenty.is.** A domain built on a generic term carries no distinctiveness either, they said, with case law to prove it. And if the register refused it, I would have to re-notarize the whole thing and pay the notary again. Rejected.

Their suggestions: put my surname in front, "Paolino Plenty Labs." It is as ugly as it reads. Or bolt on a prefix, "PG Plenty Germany." Or invent "a fantasy name, a mixture of 'plenty' and another term in one word."

So I started inventing. Is Plenty. Its Plenty. IsPlenty. ItsPlenty. My last company was called FreshFlow, so a coined compound was clearly fine. In the end I deleted the space in Plenty Labs and got **PlentyLabs**. One made-up word. That, the register would take. Weeks of back-and-forth, resolved by deleting a space.

Two real English words are too generic to name a company. Invent a meaningless one and it is distinctive enough. The rule does not reward clarity. It rewards nonsense.

Have you heard of Apple? Apple Computer, now Apple Inc. About as generic an English noun as there is, with a literal apple on the lid, and one of the most recognizable brands ever built. A name does not start out distinctive. It *becomes* distinctive because you build something worth recognizing. Germany has it backwards. It demands the distinctiveness up front, on paper, from a company that does not exist yet.

## This should have been a web form

Nobody here was lazy or hostile. The lawyers were competent. The notary did his job. The tax advisors answered carefully. The offices followed their rules. Every step was defensible on its own.

The system is the problem, not the people in it.

Germany has built a process that chains one dependency to the next, puts a fee on each, and lets a founder run up legal bills, notary bills, court fees, tax retainers, software subscriptions, and health-insurance premiums on zero revenue, all before granting the one permission a company exists for: the right to send an invoice.

Health insurance alone is about 275 euros a month. It has fallen due every month of this, against revenue of nothing.

The official reason for all of it is trust. The notary, the capital, the registers, the checks: they keep bad actors out. This is the country that produced Wirecard, a listed company that reported nearly two billion euros which did not exist. The machinery that needed five months and 9,000 euros before it would let me invoice a single client did not catch that. It only caught me.

This should have been a web form. Fill it in, pay a fee, get your company and your VAT ID in a week. Estonia does it. The UK registers a company in a day, online, for the price of a dinner. There is no law of nature that says incorporation must take five months and arrive by post. In 2026, the last thing standing between my software company and its first invoice is the German postal service.

I could have incorporated somewhere easier. I live in Germany. I wanted to build in Germany. That ought to be the easy part, not the expensive one.

Founding the company became the first product I had to finance. A company with no customers was expensive long before it was allowed to become a business. That is not a country telling builders they are welcome. It is a country taxing ambition before it earns a cent, then wondering why the ambitious leave.

151 days. 9,654 euros. Zero invoices. I am still waiting for the letter.

## Postscript: why two companies, and the 25,000-euro rule

The reasonable question is why a one-person business needs two companies. The short answer: the "simple" alternative is worse.

The simplest setup is a sole proprietorship. Thirty euros, no capital, done in an afternoon. It also makes me personally liable for everything. If a client sues, they are not suing a company. They are suing me, my savings, my home. That is not simpler. It is the same risk with less paperwork.

So I wanted real limited liability, which means a company. For one person, the cleanest shape is a partnership that does the work, with a small UG standing in as the partner that carries the liability. The partnership is taxed transparently: its profit is taxed as my income, which is honest, because I am the one who ends up with the money. A standalone GmbH would tax the company first and then tax me again when I took anything out. A UG and Co. KG is the boring, sensible answer for a solo founder, and a completely standard one here.

I picked a UG over a GmbH for the obvious reason. A GmbH demands 25,000 euros of share capital parked in the bank before you are allowed to exist. The UG lets you start with almost nothing. The catch: you then lock away 25% of your profit every year, building a reserve, until it reaches 25,000 euros, and only then convert to a GmbH. The 25,000 does not disappear. Germany just collects it slowly instead of up front.

So why 25,000 at all? It is my company and my risk. If I want to start with zero, that should be my call, not a number the state sets before it lets me try. And the cheaper path has its own price: to plenty of clients, "UG" reads as "too small to take seriously," and they would rather deal with a GmbH. The structure built to lower the barrier quietly marks you for using it.

## What was Wirecard?

If the name means nothing to you, here is the short version. Wirecard was a German payments company, and for a while the country's proudest piece of tech. It rose all the way into the DAX, Germany's top stock index, taking the place of Commerzbank. The German answer to PayPal.

In June 2020 it admitted that nearly two billion euros of cash on its balance sheet did not exist. Not misplaced. Never there. The company collapsed within days, in the largest accounting fraud in postwar German history. The chief executive was arrested. The chief operating officer fled the country and is still missing.

The worst part was the watchdogs. Germany's financial regulator had spent years defending Wirecard. When journalists and investors warned that the numbers were fake, the regulator went after them instead, filing complaints against reporters and restricting bets against the stock.

So when I am told the notaries, the capital, the registers, and the checks are all there to keep bad actors out, I think of Wirecard. The same machinery that could not spot two billion missing euros at a public company was thorough enough to make me wait five months to invoice one client. Whatever the bureaucracy is for, it is not catching the Wirecards. It is catching the founders.
