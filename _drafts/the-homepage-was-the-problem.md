For months I've been telling people about [Chat with Work](https://chatwithwork.com). I'd explain what it does, they'd get it, they'd say "oh that's actually useful." Then I'd send them the homepage and hear nothing back.

The product wasn't the problem. The homepage was.

---

The old hero said "Your all-knowing co-worker." It was a nice metaphor. It had a rainbow glow effect and everything. But it didn't answer the one question people actually have when they land on a page: *what does this thing do, specifically, for me?*

Below that was a stats section about how knowledge workers waste five hours a week searching for information. True. Also generic. Also something you've read on thirty other landing pages for thirty other products. Then a vague three-step "how it works" and some feature cards that said things like "smart results" and "talk normally." The pricing was literally blurred out behind a "coming soon" badge.

I was asking people to trust a product they couldn't see, understand a value prop that was abstract, and sign up for a waitlist with no idea what it would cost. So I rewrote every section. Not the product, not the design — just how I explain it. Here's what I learned.

## Show the question, not the feature

The old homepage had a card that said "Smart results." What does that mean? Nothing. It's a feature label without context.

The new homepage shows the actual questions you'd type:

- "What did the team decide about the pricing change?"
- "Find the latest contract from Acme Corp."
- "Catch me up on Project X before the 2pm call."

That's it. No feature name. Just the thing you'd actually do. If someone reads those and thinks "yes, I ask that question every week" — they get it. If they don't, no amount of feature cards will convince them.

## Say what you don't do

Most tools in this space ask you to sync everything first. Upload your Drive. Index your Slack. Wait a few hours. Hope the embeddings are good. You're trusting a third party with a full copy of your work data before you've even asked your first question.

Chat with Work doesn't do that. It searches your accounts directly, in real time, every time. Nothing is copied into our system. There is no second copy of your data sitting on our servers.

The old homepage never said this. I assumed people would figure out that we were different. They didn't, because I never told them. Your differentiators don't exist until they're on the page.

## Don't blur your pricing

The old pricing section had three tiers behind a blur filter with an "invite-only alpha" badge on top. I thought it looked exclusive. It actually looked like I had something to hide.

The new page shows real prices in real euros. Starter at 5, Individual at 20, Pro at 50. Simple.
## People will surprise you with use cases

I added a section with work use cases: finding contracts, prepping for meetings, drafting replies. Expected stuff. Then, after using it extensively for personal use, I added a section for it: rental contracts, electricity bills, tax documents, insurance policies.

That personal section resonates more than I expected. Turns out people have the same "I know it's in Drive somewhere" problem at home. I wouldn't have known if I hadn't put it on the page.

## The product didn't change

That's the part that's been sitting with me. Chat with Work worked before the redesign and it works now. The AI still searches your Drive in seconds. The setup is still five minutes. The answers still cite their sources. Nothing about the product is different.

What's different is that the homepage finally shows it.

I think a lot of makers get stuck here. You build something good, you know it's good because you use it every day, and you assume the homepage just needs to be vaguely appealing and people will figure it out. They won't. You have to show the thing. Specific questions, specific answers, specific prices. Show, don't tell, isn't just writing advice. It's product advice.
